The Standard

Christian Retributive Justice

The framework for evaluating governance and pursuing renewal

Before we can say what's wrong with Minnesota governance, we need a standard to measure it against. That standard is Christian retributive justice‚Äînot partisan politics, not utilitarian outcomes, but the biblical understanding of what law is for and what rulers are supposed to do.

Punishment is Backward-Looking

Punishment is for what has been done, not for engineering future outcomes or "reforming" people. The magistrate answers a real wrong already committed.

Romans 13:4 ‚Äî "He is God's servant for your good. But if you do wrong, be afraid, for he does not bear the sword in vain."

Deserved, Proportionate, Limited

Punishment must be deserved (only for real wrongs), proportionate (fitting the offense), and limited (no more than what is deserved).

Exodus 21:23-25 ‚Äî The "eye for eye" principle establishes a ceiling and measure, not a license for vengeance.

Authority is Ministerial

Civil rulers are ministers under God's law, not autonomous authorities who can invent their own standards of right and wrong.

Genesis 18:25 ‚Äî "Shall not the Judge of all the earth do what is just?"

Impartial Application

The law must apply equally‚Äîno partiality to the rich or the poor, the powerful or the weak, the politically favored or disfavored.

Leviticus 19:15 ‚Äî "You shall do no injustice in court. You shall not be partial to the poor or defer to the great."

Contrast: Utilitarian/Therapeutic Models

Modern governance often operates on a different theory: punishment is for future outcomes‚Äî deterrence, rehabilitation, "protecting society," or reshaping attitudes. Without desert as a hard limit, this becomes unbounded: any measure can seem justified if it serves the "greater good." This is how you get indefinite detention, social credit systems, debanking for wrong opinions, and "catch and release" for favored groups.

Christian retributivism says: you may only punish for genuine wrongdoing, and the punishment must not exceed what is deserved‚Äîeven if "more" might seem socially useful.

The Evaluation Rubric

We evaluate every case, policy, and actor against six dimensions:

A

Moral Wrong

Was there a real moral wrong by biblical standards?

B

Legal Clarity

Is the law clear and limited, or vague and abusable?

C

Is the penalty commensurate with the actual wrong?

D

Authority

Are actors operating ministerially or managerially?

F

Orientation

Is reasoning backward-looking (desert) or forward-looking (social engineering)?

The Goal

Renewal, Not Despair

What we're building toward‚Äîa constructive Christian alternative

The current federal administration often operates with a "liberation" instinct from progressive governance. That instinct is often correct. But Christians need to articulate what we are building toward, not just what we are tearing down.

Retributive, Not Reformist Governance

The goal of law is not to psychologically reprogram people or engineer social outcomes. It is just recompense: punish real wrongs proportionately, protect the innocent, and leave people free to live under ordered liberty.

Physical Economy Over Financialization

Prioritize mining, energy, manufacturing, agriculture‚Äîthings that produce real goods and employ real people. Develop domestic energy (nuclear, natural gas, critical minerals) rather than depending on hostile foreign powers.

Humanitarian Programs That Work

Care for the vulnerable is a Christian duty, but programs must be effective and just, not bureaucratic slush funds. VA care with accountability. Healthcare with cost transparency. Welfare with work requirements. Every dollar tracked.

Fiscal Responsibility

Do not steal from future generations via debt. Fund only what is necessary and just. No corporate welfare or crony capitalism. Sound money and balanced budgets.

What Renewal Looks Like Institutionally

  • Clear statutes: Laws that define real wrongs precisely, not vague tools for political enforcement
  • Proportionate penalties: Punishments that fit the crime‚Äîno more, no less
  • Impartial enforcement: The law applies equally regardless of political identity
  • Limited authority: Rulers are ministers, not engineers; they answer to God's law
  • Protection of life: From conception to natural death, the innocent are protected
  • Religious liberty: Churches, schools, and families free to live by their convictions
  • Strong families: Policy supports marriage and parental authority
